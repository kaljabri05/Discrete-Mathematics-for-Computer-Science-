Discrete Mathematics for Computer Science

This guide covers Propositional Logic and Predicate Logic, fundamental concepts in discrete mathematics for computer science. Explore propositions, logical operators, quantifiers, and rules of inference with clear explanations and examples.

1. Propositional Logic

Proposition: A declarative sentence that is either true (T) or false (F), but not both. Propositional variables are denoted by letters like \( p, q, r, \ldots \).

Examples of Propositions:

  • "Today is Friday." (True or False depending on the day)
  • "Two plus two equals four." (Always True)

Non-Propositions:

  • "He is a college student?" (Question, not declarative)
  • "\( x + y > 0 \)" (Depends on variables, not fixed)

Propositional Calculus: The study of propositions and how they combine using logical operators.

1.1 Compound Propositions and Logical Operators

Compound propositions are formed by combining propositions using logical operators.

Fundamental Logical Operators

  • Negation (\( \neg p \)): "Not \( p \)", true when \( p \) is false.
  • Conjunction (\( p \land q \)): "\( p \) AND \( q \)", true when both \( p \) and \( q \) are true.
  • Disjunction (\( p \lor q \)): "\( p \) OR \( q \)", true when at least one of \( p \) or \( q \) is true (inclusive OR).
  • Exclusive OR (\( p \oplus q \)): True when exactly one of \( p \) or \( q \) is true.
  • Conditional (\( p \rightarrow q \)): "If \( p \), then \( q \)", false only when \( p \) is true and \( q \) is false.
  • Biconditional (\( p \leftrightarrow q \)): "\( p \) if and only if \( q \)", true when \( p \) and \( q \) have the same truth value.

Negation Examples

Proposition: "Michael's PC runs Linux." (\( p \))

Negation: \( \neg p \): "Michael's PC does not run Linux."

Proposition: "Vandana's smartphone has at least 32GB of memory." (\( q \))

Negation: \( \neg q \): "Vandana's smartphone has less than 32GB of memory."

Conjunction and Disjunction Examples

Let:

  • \( p \): "Rebecca's PC has more than 16GB free hard disk space."
  • \( q \): "The processor in Rebecca's PC runs faster than 1GHz."

Conjunction: \( p \land q \): "Rebecca's PC has more than 16GB free hard disk space, and the processor runs faster than 1GHz."

Disjunction: \( p \lor q \): "Rebecca's PC has more than 16GB free hard disk space, or the processor runs faster than 1GHz."

1.3 Logical Equivalences

Two propositions are logically equivalent (\( p \equiv q \)) if they have the same truth values for all possible inputs.

De Morgan's Laws:

\[ \neg (p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q \]

\[ \neg (p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q \]

Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence:

\[ p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg p \lor q \]

Distributive Laws:

\[ p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r) \]

\[ p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r) \]

Example: Show that \( \neg (p \rightarrow q) \equiv p \land \neg q \).

\[ \begin{aligned} \neg (p \rightarrow q) &\equiv \neg (\neg p \lor q) && \text{(Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence)} \\ &\equiv \neg (\neg p) \land \neg q && \text{(De Morgan's Law)} \\ &\equiv p \land \neg q && \text{(Double Negation Law)} \end{aligned} \]

2. Predicate Logic

Predicate: A property that a subject can have, expressed as a statement involving one or more variables. Denoted as \( P(x) \), where \( P \) is the predicate and \( x \) is the subject variable.

Once a value is assigned to \( x \), \( P(x) \) becomes a proposition with a truth value.

Example: Let \( P(x) \) be "\( x \) is greater than 3".

  • \( P(4) \): True, because \( 4 > 3 \).
  • \( P(2) \): False, because \( 2 \not> 3 \).

Example with Multiple Variables: Let \( Q(x, y) \) be "\( x = y + 3 \)".

  • \( Q(1, 2) \): False, because \( 1 \neq 2 + 3 \).
  • \( Q(3, 0) \): True, because \( 3 = 0 + 3 \).

Example with Three Variables: Let \( R(x, y, z) \) be "\( x + y = z \)".

  • \( R(1, 2, 3) \): True, because \( 1 + 2 = 3 \).
  • \( R(0, 0, 1) \): False, because \( 0 + 0 \neq 1 \).

2.1 Quantifiers

Quantifiers express the extent to which a predicate is true over a domain (universe of discourse).

Universal Quantifier (\( \forall \))

Universal Quantification: \( \forall x P(x) \) means "\( P(x) \) is true for all \( x \) in the domain." A single counterexample where \( P(x) \) is false disproves \( \forall x P(x) \).

Example: Let \( P(x) \) be "\( x + 1 > x \)", domain: all real numbers.

\( \forall x P(x) \): True, because adding 1 to any real number \( x \) results in a number greater than \( x \).

Example: Let \( Q(x) \) be "\( x < 2 \)", domain: all real numbers.

\( \forall x Q(x) \): False, because \( x = 5 \) is a counterexample (\( 5 \not< 2 \)).

Example: Let \( P(x) \) be "\( x^2 > 0 \)", domain: all integers.

\( \forall x P(x) \): False, because \( x = 0 \) is a counterexample (\( 0^2 = 0 \not> 0 \)).

Existential Quantifier (\( \exists \))

Existential Quantification: \( \exists x P(x) \) means "There exists an \( x \) in the domain such that \( P(x) \) is true." It is false only if \( P(x) \) is false for all \( x \).

Example: Let \( P(x) \) be "\( x > 3 \)", domain: all real numbers.

\( \exists x P(x) \): True, because there exist numbers like \( x = 4 \) where \( 4 > 3 \).

Example: Let \( Q(x) \) be "\( x = x + 1 \)", domain: all real numbers.

\( \exists x Q(x) \): False, because no real number satisfies \( x = x + 1 \).

Uniqueness Quantifier (\( \exists! \))

Uniqueness Quantification: \( \exists! x P(x) \) means "There exists exactly one \( x \) such that \( P(x) \) is true."

Quantifiers over Finite Domains

For a finite domain \( \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\} \):

  • \( \forall x P(x) \equiv P(x_1) \land P(x_2) \land \cdots \land P(x_n) \)
  • \( \exists x P(x) \equiv P(x_1) \lor P(x_2) \lor \cdots \lor P(x_n) \)

Restricted Domains

Quantifiers can be restricted, e.g., \( \forall x < 0 (x^2 > 0) \) means "\( x^2 > 0 \) for all \( x < 0 \)".

Negating Quantified Expressions

De Morgan's Laws for Quantifiers:

\[ \neg (\forall x P(x)) \equiv \exists x \neg P(x) \]

\[ \neg (\exists x P(x)) \equiv \forall x \neg P(x) \]

Example: Negate \( \forall x \exists y (xy = 1) \).

\[ \begin{aligned} \neg (\forall x \exists y (xy = 1)) &\equiv \exists x \neg (\exists y (xy = 1)) \\ &\equiv \exists x \forall y \neg (xy = 1) \\ &\equiv \exists x \forall y (xy \neq 1) \end{aligned} \]

Nested Quantifiers

Nested quantifiers involve one quantifier within the scope of another.

Example: Let \( Q(x, y) \) be "\( x + y = 0 \)", domain: real numbers.

  • \( \exists y \forall x Q(x, y) \): There exists a \( y \) such that \( x + y = 0 \) for all \( x \). False, because no single \( y \) works for all \( x \).
  • \( \forall x \exists y Q(x, y) \): For every \( x \), there exists a \( y \) such that \( x + y = 0 \). True, because for any \( x \), we can choose \( y = -x \).

Note: The order of quantifiers matters!

Translating Quantified Statements

Example: Translate "The sum of two positive integers is always positive."

Logical expression: \( \forall x \forall y ((x > 0) \land (y > 0) \rightarrow (x + y > 0)) \), where \( x, y \in \mathbb{Z} \).

If the domain is positive integers: \( \forall x \forall y (x + y > 0) \), where \( x, y \in \mathbb{N} \).

3. Rules of Inference

An argument is a sequence of propositions where the final proposition (conclusion) follows from the premises. An argument is valid if the premises being true guarantees the conclusion is true.

3.1 Propositional Logic Rules

Modus Ponens:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \rightarrow q \\ p \\ \hline q \end{aligned} \]

Modus Tollens:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \rightarrow q \\ \neg q \\ \hline \neg p \end{aligned} \]

Hypothetical Syllogism:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \rightarrow q \\ q \rightarrow r \\ \hline p \rightarrow r \end{aligned} \]

Disjunctive Syllogism:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \lor q \\ \neg p \\ \hline q \end{aligned} \]

Addition:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \\ \hline p \lor q \end{aligned} \]

Simplification:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \land q \\ \hline p \end{aligned} \]

Conjunction:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \\ q \\ \hline p \land q \end{aligned} \]

Resolution:

\[ \begin{aligned} p \lor q \\ \neg p \lor r \\ \hline q \lor r \end{aligned} \]

3.2 Fallacies

Fallacies are errors in reasoning that lead to invalid arguments.

Fallacy of Affirming the Conclusion (Converse Error):

\[ \begin{aligned} p \rightarrow q \\ q \\ \hline p \end{aligned} \]

Fallacy of Denying the Hypothesis (Inverse Error):

\[ \begin{aligned} p \rightarrow q \\ \neg p \\ \hline \neg q \end{aligned} \]

3.3 Quantified Statement Rules

Universal Instantiation:

\[ \begin{aligned} \forall x P(x) \\ \hline P(c) \end{aligned} \]

Universal Generalization:

\[ \begin{aligned} P(c) \text{ for an arbitrary } c \\ \hline \forall x P(x) \end{aligned} \]

Existential Instantiation:

\[ \begin{aligned} \exists x P(x) \\ \hline P(c) \text{ for some element } c \end{aligned} \]

Existential Generalization:

\[ \begin{aligned} P(c) \text{ for some element } c \\ \hline \exists x P(x) \end{aligned} \]

Universal Modus Ponens:

\[ \begin{aligned} \forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x)) \\ P(a) \\ \hline Q(a) \end{aligned} \]

Universal Modus Tollens:

\[ \begin{aligned} \forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x)) \\ \neg Q(a) \\ \hline \neg P(a) \end{aligned} \]
