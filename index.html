1. Introduction to Propositional Logic

Proposition: A declarative sentence that is either true (T) or false (F), but not both. Propositional variables are denoted by letters like \( p, q, r, \ldots \).

Examples of Propositions:

  • "Today is Friday." (True or False depending on the day)
  • "Two plus two equals four." (Always True)

Non-Propositions:

  • "He is a college student?" (Question, not declarative)
  • "\( x + y > 0 \)" (Depends on variables, not fixed)

Propositional Calculus: The study of propositions and how they combine using logical operators.

2. Compound Propositions and Logical Operators

Compound propositions are formed by combining propositions using logical operators.

Fundamental Logical Operators

  • Negation (\( \neg p \)): "Not \( p \)", true when \( p \) is false.
  • Conjunction (\( p \land q \)): "\( p \) AND \( q \)", true when both \( p \) and \( q \) are true.
  • Disjunction (\( p \lor q \)): "\( p \) OR \( q \)", true when at least one of \( p \) or \( q \) is true (inclusive OR).
  • Exclusive OR (\( p \oplus q \)): True when exactly one of \( p \) or \( q \) is true.
  • Conditional (\( p \rightarrow q \)): "If \( p \), then \( q \)", false only when \( p \) is true and \( q \) is false.
  • Biconditional (\( p \leftrightarrow q \)): "\( p \) if and only if \( q \)", true when \( p \) and \( q \) have the same truth value.

Negation Examples

Proposition: "Michael's PC runs Linux." (\( p \))

Negation: \( \neg p \): "Michael's PC does not run Linux."

Proposition: "Vandana's smartphone has at least 32GB of memory." (\( q \))

Negation: \( \neg q \): "Vandana's smartphone has less than 32GB of memory."

Conjunction and Disjunction Examples

Let:

  • \( p \): "Rebecca's PC has more than 16GB free hard disk space."
  • \( q \): "The processor in Rebecca's PC runs faster than 1GHz."

Conjunction: \( p \land q \): "Rebecca's PC has more than 16GB free hard disk space, and the processor runs faster than 1GHz."

Disjunction: \( p \lor q \): "Rebecca's PC has more than 16GB free hard disk space, or the processor runs faster than 1GHz."

The inclusive OR (\( \lor \)) is true when at least one proposition is true, while the exclusive OR (\( \oplus \)) is true when exactly one proposition is true.

Conditional Statement

A conditional statement \( p \rightarrow q \) ("If \( p \), then \( q \)") is false only when \( p \) is true and \( q \) is false.

Example: If \( x = 0 \) before the statement "If \( 2+2=4 \), then \( x = x + 1 \)", then after the statement, \( x = 1 \), because \( 2+2=4 \) is true, so the consequent is executed.

Converse, Contrapositive, and Inverse

For a conditional statement \( p \rightarrow q \):

  • Converse: \( q \rightarrow p \)
  • Contrapositive: \( \neg q \rightarrow \neg p \)
  • Inverse: \( \neg p \rightarrow \neg q \)

Statement: "The home team wins whenever it is raining." (\( p \rightarrow q \))

  • Converse: If the home team wins, then it is raining.
  • Contrapositive: If the home team does not win, then it is not raining.
  • Inverse: If it is not raining, then the home team does not win.

Note: The contrapositive has the same truth value as the original statement.

5. Precedence of Logical Operators

Logical operators follow this order of precedence:

  1. \( \neg \) (Negation)
  2. \( \land \) (Conjunction)
  3. \( \lor \) (Disjunction)
  4. \( \rightarrow \) (Conditional)
  5. \( \leftrightarrow \) (Biconditional)

6. Logical Equivalences

Two propositions are logically equivalent (\( p \equiv q \)) if they have the same truth values for all possible inputs (i.e., \( p \leftrightarrow q \) is a tautology).

Key Logical Equivalences

De Morgan's Laws:

\[ \neg (p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q \]

\[ \neg (p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q \]

Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence:

\[ p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg p \lor q \]

Distributive Laws:

\[ p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r) \]

\[ p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r) \]

Identity Laws:

\[ p \land T \equiv p \]

\[ p \lor F \equiv p \]

Domination Laws:

\[ p \lor T \equiv T \]

\[ p \land F \equiv F \]

Proving Logical Equivalences

Example 1: Show that \( \neg (p \rightarrow q) \equiv p \land \neg q \).

\[ \begin{aligned} \neg (p \rightarrow q) &\equiv \neg (\neg p \lor q) && \text{(Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence)} \\ &\equiv \neg (\neg p) \land \neg q && \text{(De Morgan's Law)} \\ &\equiv p \land \neg q && \text{(Double Negation Law)} \end{aligned} \]

Example 2: Show that \( \neg (p \lor (\neg p \land q)) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q \).

\[ \begin{aligned} \neg (p \lor (\neg p \land q)) &\equiv \neg p \land \neg (\neg p \land q) && \text{(De Morgan's Law)} \\ &\equiv \neg p \land [\neg (\neg p) \lor \neg q] && \text{(De Morgan's Law)} \\ &\equiv \neg p \land (p \lor \neg q) && \text{(Double Negation Law)} \\ &\equiv (\neg p \land p) \lor (\neg p \land \neg q) && \text{(Distributive Law)} \\ &\equiv F \lor (\neg p \land \neg q) && \text{(Negation Law)} \\ &\equiv (\neg p \land \neg q) \lor F && \text{(Commutative Law)} \\ &\equiv \neg p \land \neg q && \text{(Identity Law)} \end{aligned} \]
